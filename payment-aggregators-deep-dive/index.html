Payment Aggregators Deep Dive

    In one of our previous posts on Payment Aggregators we wrote an introduction, in this article, we dive a little deeper.

    One of the sole purposes of a payment aggregator is to provide a streamlined payment solution that is a shortcut from traditional payment methods.

    Who is a Payment Aggregator?

    Payment Aggregators refer to the following institutions:

    Technology providers who route and facilitate the processing of an online payment transaction without actually handling the funds.

    Entities that help e-commerce sites and merchants in accepting various payment instruments from customers.

    Entities who aid the merchants in connecting with the acquirers.

    What are the pre-requisites for being a Payment Aggregator?

    A payment aggregator needs to have a payment aggregator license and necessary certification from the Payment Card Industry (Data Security Standard/ PCI DSS).

    RBI recently published guidelines, payment gateway and payment aggregator guidelines.

    What documents are required to obtain Payment Aggregator License?

    The documents required to obtain a payment Aggregator License are as follows:

    Certificate of incorporation of Company received from Registrar of Companies (ROC).

    PAN Card or Address proof of the Directors.

    DSC and DIN of the directors.

    Address proof of the place of business.

    Details of the Bank Account of the Company.

    Business plan of the Company for five years.

    Code testing report by a software agency.

    What are the Benefits of Payment Aggregator Business Model for Merchants?

    The business model is usually where a SaaS company acts as a master merchant for its users. The SaaS provider onboards its clients via a fairly non-intrusive application process. In most cases, SaaS users can begin accepting payments within an hour of applying.

    This application process collects enough information for required due diligence ( KYC ), however, once complete, there are significant compliance controls and checks for which the SaaS provider is responsible.

    Along with payment integration, compliance, merchant funding, and risk compliance demands, there is a significant amount of time and money that must be invested to make payment aggregation possible.

    The merchant pays a comission only when a customer buys.

    The merchant saves marketing and distribution cost for the product.

    How does the end-user benefit in Aggregator Business Model?

    Needs : The aggregators help customers to filter selection based on their needs.

    Trust : The platform aggregates reviews from a multitude of customers, and helps arrive the customers to choose a trusted product or service.

    Cost : By doing a price comparison the end-user buys the product that fits his / her budget.

    What are the typical Aggregator Business Models?

    Merchant of Record Model

    The entity that is authorized, and held liable, by a financial institution to process a consumer’s credit and debit card transactions.

    MoR assumes financial responsibility for its clients.

    example includes Square Payments

    The Payment Facilitator Model

    Visa defines a payment facilitator as a third-party agent that may do the following: Sign a merchant acceptance agreement on behalf of an acquirer. Receive settlement of transaction proceeds from an acquirer, on behalf of a sub-merchant.

    Here is a list of MasterCard PayFacs

    Marketplace Model

    The marketplace is a newly emerged concept, defined by Visa, which provides an electronic commerce website or app to bring the consumers and sellers in the same platform. Also, it manages payments in aid of the retailers on a marketplace.

    Staged-Digital Wallet Operator Model

    A company that stands upon the Staged-Digital Wallet (SDWO) model offers digital wallet service. The services offered include a transaction facility that can be made via credit/debit card to the SDWO, and processed through the account of its merchant.

    Examples include PayTM Wallet, Amazon Wallet, and others.

    How Payment Aggregators Make Money?

    A payment aggregator relies on backend banks to enable payment services to merchant so the aggregator has to pay to those banks.

    Typical cost structure for e-commerce merchants:

    Debit Cards (below Rs.2000): No Cost

    Debit Cards (Above Rs.2000): Differential pricing for On-Us and Off-us cards (in percentage, %)

    Credit Cards: Differential pricing for On-Us and Off-Us cards (in percentage, %)

    Amex: in % and higher than other cards

    Net-banking: Either fixed cost in percentage (%) of value or revenue sharing (60:40 or 50:50)

    Other payment methods: in percentage (%) of value

    Where do Aggregators lose money?

    Refunds due to online transaction failure.

    Refudns due to product quality issues.

    What is the current scenario for the Aggregator industry?

    Today, the aggregation business is booming. Cellulant in Kenya currently connects 14 banks into the M-PESA mobile money service for a total value of $70 million pass-through value per month. Pegasus in Uganda processes nearly 200,000 electricity payments per month totaling $10 million in pass-through value. While these figures sound impressive, they do not automatically translate into high profits. Aggregators require significant volumes just to break even.

    Aggregators are now facing intense competition from their own partners in terms of Technical Capability, and Revenue Margins.

    How does the Future look like for Aggregator industry?

    Digital Lending Products

    Aggregators are also developing credit scoring algorithms and are trying to position themselves in the emerging market for digital credit products. There are many verticals, including education and entertainment, that are in need of digital financial services to enhance their customer offerings. Food, Retail, and School Chains are some of the early targets.

    Own Ciritical Piece of Value Chain

    The aggregator business model starts with plugging into various sources of supply. The supply could be Drivers(Uber), or rooms(AirBnB), couriers(DoorDash), professionals(Thumbtack) and forth. The second step as we see is that they build supply with direct relationship with suppliers. For instance AirBnB faces competitoin from the likes of Expedia, and finally to beat the competition they become the supply.

    Finally they face stiff competition and offer the supply. An example of an aggregator becoming a supply is eBay launching “eBay Buy APIs” in 2016, though not massively successful it geared $1 Bn in GMV. Expedia and Booking Holdings (Priceline) expose their supply to apps via APIs.

    Aggregators always start with plugging into supply, and eventually evolving into Supply Owners.
